    Executive summary

    The right amendment is to treat the Igloo as a high-context briefing and the workshop as an artifact-production day. AnaCredit itself is the ECB’s granular credit dataset for the euro area, governed by Regulation (EU) 2016/867; the ECB’s Manual and Q&As support interpretation but do not create additional legal obligations, while national implementation details are explicitly left to the relevant national central bank. For Luxembourg, that makes the Banque centrale du Luxembourg operationally authoritative for reporting dates, validation rules, comparison methodologies, and SDMX schemas. [1]

    BiL’s own materials describe a current-state AnaCredit process that is manual, multi-system, and correction-heavy: source data arrives from T24, RFO Master, and branch or subsidiary inputs; staging and upload are manual; INVOKE and regulator feedback create rework loops; and downstream corrections are still handled case by case. The same materials position the target state as automated ingestion, deterministic controls in OPA/Policy-as-Code, AI-assisted remediation, human-in-the-loop approval, structured downstream feedback, and full evidence capture. The project brief frames the value hypothesis as a 50–70%+ reduction in monthly manual effort and cycle time, with better first-pass acceptance, lower defect leakage, and complete audit evidence by default. fileciteturn0file3 fileciteturn0file4 fileciteturn0file2

    The main analytical implication is that engineering should not be asked to discover the operating model while it is also building the platform. The uploaded draft pack already identifies the highest-risk unknowns correctly: rule authority, data ownership, exception governance, observed-agent scope, reconciliation ownership, and evidence-pack design. Those are not “later hardening” items. They are the preconditions for a credible build plan. fileciteturn0file0

    This amendment pack therefore converts the Igloo’s six closing lenses—Value, Data, Policy, Controls, People, Evidence—into six timed workshop activities with named outputs. It also adds the engineering handoff layer missing from many workshop decks: editable artifact templates, a reusable Policy-as-Code process schema, synthetic interview transcripts to accelerate prep, detailed as-is and to-be service blueprints, demo acceptance criteria, a Now/Next/Later backlog, and a ready-to-print collateral pack. The structure is intentionally aligned with the Igloo’s narrative arc and the BiL demo promise of one-click orchestration, OPA/PAC governance, and full chain of custody. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2 fileciteturn0file4

    Regulatory frame and design principles

    AnaCredit should be framed in the workshop as a regulated data operating model, not only a reporting obligation. Officially, AnaCredit is a harmonized euro-area dataset containing detailed information on individual bank loans across Member States. The ECB explains that the Regulation defines the reporting requirements and population; the Manual provides guidance; the Q&As complement the Manual; and the national implementation details, including timeliness and format, are determined by the relevant NCB. That hierarchy matters because it is the difference between a defensible control rule and a convenient but ungrounded automation. [2]

    The official data model is also more structured than many workshop discussions assume. AnaCredit currently focuses on loans to corporations and other legal entities, mostly on a monthly basis. The Manual makes clear that natural persons are outside the standard reporting logic unless other debtors on the same instrument are not natural persons; it also shows the continuing importance of the €25,000 reporting threshold. Just as importantly for BiL, the Manual explains that a reporting agent can comprise multiple observed agents across countries and that the logical data model expands into six entity tables connected by keys and bridge tables. That gives the Data lens a precise workshop target: trace one exposure through instrument, counterparty, protection, and linkage entities, not through a generic “loan record.” [3]

    The official data-quality architecture is three-layered. The ECB’s validation checks document groups the common deterministic checks around referential integrity, completeness, and consistency, and it notes that reporting agents are encouraged to strengthen their data quality management systems. The ECB also states that NCBs may perform additional validation checks beyond the common minimum standard. Separately, the ECB’s AnaCredit hub explains that plausibility checks aim to identify data that are likely to be incorrect and therefore require verification. For design purposes, that means the workshop should never collapse deterministic checks, plausibility review, and reconciliation into one undifferentiated “AI validation” bucket. [4]

    Luxembourg implementation details are active enough that versioning must be treated as a day-one requirement. As of the current BCL instructions page, the stack includes reporting dates, reporting-agent lists, Member State lists, validation rules, comparison methodologies, and the SDMX technical stack. The same page shows v1.0.13 as mandatory from the January 2026 reference date through June 2026, and v1.0.14 as mandatory from the July 2026 reference date onward. BCL also distinguishes contact channels for credit data and referential data, which is a strong operational clue for exception taxonomy and ownership design. [5]

    The BCL reconciliation process adds another workshop constraint. The BCL methodology for the AnaCredit/FINREP comparison says the ECB and NCBs introduced a quarterly comparison report to evaluate consistency and completeness; reporting agents in scope are expected to correct discrepancies; the feedback workbook includes an Overview sheet and can also include Included and Excluded instrument sheets; and revision deadlines are 20 working days for the first feedback and 5 days for later feedback, with email confirmation before resubmission. This means reconciliation and feedback handling cannot be an afterthought confined to a later phase. The workshop should prepare sample comparison artifacts and root-cause routing up front. [6]

    The supervisory context also supports an evidence-first design. In February 2026, ECB Banking Supervision sanctioned J.P. Morgan SE €12.18 million for misreporting capital requirements, explicitly describing the breaches as serious negligence driven by deficiencies in internal processes and controls. That case was not about AnaCredit, but it is current, official evidence that weak reporting controls can become material supervisory events. For the broader “agentic bank” agenda, the EU Commission’s AI Act overview is also relevant: for high-risk AI systems, the Commission highlights obligations around logging, documentation, human oversight, robustness, cybersecurity, and accuracy. AnaCredit may not itself map neatly to an Annex III “high-risk” category in the way that credit scoring does, but those controls are a prudent baseline for a regulated agentic workflow. Finally, because approval logs, user actions, and decision histories can contain personal data, GDPR and EU data-protection governance remain applicable to the evidence layer. [7]

    The workshop should therefore be run against five design principles. Every automated rule must carry an authority label and version. Every material reported value must be traceable to a source field, mapper, and steward. Deterministic validation, plausibility review, and reconciliation must be modeled as separate control layers. Human approval and evidence capture must be explicit at each control gate. And schema/version changes must be treated as release management problems, not as documentation footnotes. [9]

    Lens activities and editable artifacts

    The Igloo already ends on six lenses. The amendment is to make each lens resolve into a named artifact rather than a general conversation. That structure is also consistent with the uploaded draft package, which prioritizes executive framing, persona/RACI, observed-agent scope, journey and failure taxonomy, data inventory and lineage, rule authority, exception decisions, identifier policy, golden data, API/schema contracts, acceptance criteria, and governance checklists before engineering. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file0

    
    Value

    This activity is anchored in the project brief’s value hypothesis and the Igloo’s “Regulatory Pressure” scene: cycle time, first-pass acceptance, defect leakage, automation coverage, and evidence completeness are the right scorecard dimensions for the first build. fileciteturn0file4 fileciteturn0file1

    Timebox: 50–60 minutes

    Purpose: Convert the business challenge into a measurable value case that can later become demo KPIs, steering metrics, and phase-exit criteria.

    Prompts:
“What must be materially better after the first release?”
“What is the strongest value claim BiL can defend externally and internally?”
“Which metrics are leading indicators versus lagging indicators?”

    Facilitation steps: 1. Ask for two minutes of silent writing: each participant writes the three outcomes that would justify funding the build. 2. Consolidate proposed outcomes into one board and cluster them under speed, quality, control, and trust. 3. Walk the group through the Value Scorecard template and fill baseline confidence first: known, estimated, unknown. 4. Force target language into measurable terms. If the room says “better quality,” ask, “measured how?” 5. Mark three sponsor decisions that require leadership commitment rather than analysis. 6. Close by reading the scorecard aloud and asking for verbal confirmation from the executive sponsor and reporting lead.

    Required materials: Value Scorecard canvas, printed current-state metrics if available, dot stickers, timer, decision log sheet.

    Exact outputs: A filled Value Scorecard; a list of missing baselines to collect; three sponsor decisions requiring follow-up.

    
    
    Data

    This activity combines the current-state breakpoint map with the one-loan test. It is grounded in the BiL current process and in the official AnaCredit object model, which is built around instruments, counterparties, protections, and bridge entities rather than a single flat record. fileciteturn0file3 fileciteturn0file1 [10]

    Timebox: 70–75 minutes

    Purpose: Make the invisible current-state failure points tangible and prove whether the bank can trace one representative exposure through systems, transformations, validations, and owners.

    Prompts:
“Where does the current process lose time, context, or evidence?”
“For one specific exposure, what data must be trusted before submission can ever be trusted?”
“Which defects are source problems, which are mapping problems, and which are interpretation problems?”

    Facilitation steps: 1. Place the current-state flow on the wall and ask participants to tag breakpoints using pre-printed failure cards. 2. Group the tags into five defect classes: source, reference, mapping, rule, and submission handling. 3. Select one representative exposure for the session; ideally use the same stylized loan shown in the Igloo. 4. Trace that exposure across instrument, counterparty, and protection data using the One-Loan Trace template. 5. For each critical element, record the source, transformation, current steward, and present evidence. 6. End by asking which fields cannot yet be automated safely because lineage or stewardship is still unclear.

    Required materials: Breakpoint Map canvas, current-state process print, sample loan pack, One-Loan Trace template, system legend, sticky notes, pens.

    Exact outputs: A filled Breakpoint Map; a One-Loan Trace; a prioritized list of lineage and stewardship gaps.

    
    
    Policy

    This activity exists because the official source hierarchy is layered: binding regulation, ECB guidance, national implementation, internal policy, and AI assistance are not the same thing. It also needs to respect the ECB distinction between deterministic validation and plausibility review, while treating OPA as the execution engine for the deterministic layer. [11]

    Timebox: 60–70 minutes

    Purpose: Turn policy ambiguity into a first-pass catalog of executable, reviewable, and escalation-only rules.

    Prompts:
“What is the authority for this rule?”
“Is this rule deterministic, plausibility-based, reconciliation-based, or interpretive?”
“What evidence would prove that the rule passed, failed, or was overridden?”

    Facilitation steps: 1. Start with a two-minute refresher of the source hierarchy: Regulation, Manual/Q&A, BCL, internal policy, AI suggestion. 2. Capture candidate rules from the current pain points and existing validation logic. 3. Classify each rule into deterministic block, deterministic warning, plausibility indicator, reconciliation indicator, or human interpretation. 4. Require a source citation or source type for every rule. If the room cannot name it, mark the rule “authority unresolved.” 5. Record owner, approver, evidence object, and test-case needs. 6. Close by identifying which rules are safe to put into the first OPA release and which require a prior policy decision.

    Required materials: Rule sorting canvas, printed source hierarchy card, Policy-as-Code Rule Catalogue template, markers, sample validation errors.

    Exact outputs: A completed draft Rule Catalogue; an unresolved-interpretation list; a shortlist of first-release deterministic rules.

    
    
    Controls

    This activity is the operational translation of BiL’s target state. The target architecture and demo materials consistently show a human-in-the-loop workflow with OPA/PAC validation, one-click orchestration, and structured approval rather than autonomous black-box behavior. fileciteturn0file1 fileciteturn0file2 fileciteturn0file3 fileciteturn0file4

    Timebox: 55–65 minutes

    Purpose: Define the control gates that separate automated system action from accountable human decision.

    Prompts:
“What must be true before submission is even possible?”
“What can the system do without approval, and what can it only recommend?”
“What is the formal override path when a deadline and a control conflict?”

    Facilitation steps: 1. Put the target-state flow on the wall and ask the room to mark every point where a decision changes the submission outcome. 2. Turn those moments into named gates: ingestion, schema validation, deterministic rule pass, exception review, attestation, submission. 3. For each gate, define entry conditions, blocking rules, approver, override path, SLA, and evidence captured. 4. Walk one failure scenario through the whole model to test whether ownership and timing are realistic. 5. Mark any gate that still depends on unwritten internal policy. 6. Close by naming the minimum control UI fields that must be visible in the demo.

    Required materials: Control Gate Model canvas, target-state flow print, red/amber/green stickers, exception scenarios, timer.

    Exact outputs: A completed Control Gate Model; an exception and override map; a control-UI must-show list.

    
    
    People

    This activity formalizes decision rights under regulatory time pressure. It should be grounded in the actual operating chain implied by BiL’s current flow, the observed-agent logic in the ECB Manual, and the practical split between credit-data and referential-data ownership visible in Luxembourg’s contact structure. fileciteturn0file3 [12]

    Timebox: 45–55 minutes

    Purpose: Replace diffuse accountability with explicit decision rights, remediation ownership, and escalation logic.

    Prompts:
“When the future system finds a problem, who decides, who fixes, who approves, and who is informed?”
“Which conflicts must be escalated rather than solved peer-to-peer?”
“What is each persona’s trust threshold for AI assistance?”

    Facilitation steps: 1. Put the core personas on the wall and ask participants to add missing roles or committees. 2. Fill the Persona/RACI Map for the key decisions: rule interpretation, exception approval, source correction, submission approval, feedback closure. 3. Stress-test the matrix with two scenarios: a blocking deterministic failure and a high-value yellow reconciliation issue near deadline. 4. Record unresolved organizational questions as explicit governance actions, not side notes. 5. Ask each function to state the minimum evidence they would need to trust the system. 6. Close by identifying who must attend future sign-off sessions and who only needs readouts.

    Required materials: Persona cards, RACI template, escalation matrix worksheet, markers, dot stickers.

    Exact outputs: A filled Persona/RACI Map; a trust-threshold summary by role; a governance open-questions log.

    
    
    Evidence

    This activity makes auditability concrete. It synthesizes BiL’s own chain-of-custody promise with the wider control logic implied by the AI Act’s logging and human-oversight requirements and by GDPR/data-protection obligations around personal-data handling in logs and approval trails. fileciteturn0file2 fileciteturn0file4 [13]

    Timebox: 50–60 minutes

    Purpose: Specify the “trust record” the system must produce for reporting, audit, and supervision.

    Prompts:
“If a regulator, audit team, or executive asks why this submission was trusted, what must the system show immediately?”
“What evidence can be captured automatically, and what still requires explicit attestation?”
“What evidence is materially incomplete if policy version, schema version, or approval reason is missing?”

    Facilitation steps: 1. List every event that changes trust status: data arrival, rule execution, suggestion, approval, override, submission, feedback intake. 2. For each event, define the mandatory fields, whether capture is automatic, whether personal data may be involved, and who may see it. 3. Build the Trust Record row by row until the full submission run can be replayed from it. 4. Use the resulting evidence model to draft the demo contract: what the first demo must visibly prove. 5. Mark retention and privacy questions for security/legal follow-up. 6. Close by reading back the minimum evidence pack that would satisfy reporting, audit, and engineering.

    Required materials: Trust Record template, evidence card set, data-protection prompt sheet, Demo Contract template, timer.

    Exact outputs: A completed Trust Record; a first-pass Demo Contract; a privacy/retention action list.

    Synthetic interview transcripts

    The transcripts below are deliberately synthetic. They are not records of actual BiL interviews. They are workshop-ready composites grounded in the BiL current-state and target-state materials, the official ECB/BCL control structure, and the role and ownership tensions that those sources imply. Their purpose is to help workshop leads prepare, seed role-play, test assumptions, and accelerate live interview planning. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file3 [15]

    
    Head of Regulatory Reporting

    Q: What turns the monthly run into an executive issue rather than an operations issue?
A: The report is the visible end of the chain. By the time the bank is discussing corrections, the reporting team owns the deadline, even if the cause sits upstream. That is when an operational defect becomes a governance problem.

    Q: What is the hardest thing to explain to senior management today?
A: That a single rejection code can hide three different causes: bad source data, bad mapping, or a policy interpretation problem. From the outside, they all look like “reporting got it wrong.”

    Q: What would a genuinely useful control screen show you?
A: I need the failed rule, its severity, the source lineage, the current owner, the previous treatment, and the approval status. If I still need three calls to understand a red item, the UI has not solved the problem.

    Q: What evidence do you need before approving submission?
A: I need to know which blockers were resolved, which warnings remain, who approved the exceptions, what rule set ran, and whether the file is reproducible from the captured snapshot.

    Q: What is your trust threshold for AI assistance?
A: AI can recommend and summarize. It can help me see patterns and propose options. It cannot silently alter a reportable value without an explicit human decision and a traceable reason.

    Q: What would make you call the first release successful?
A: Fewer late escalations, fewer regulator returns, faster triage, and a clean evidence pack every cycle. Efficiency is welcome, but defensibility is the real signal.

    
    
    AnaCredit Ops Lead

    Q: What work feels most repetitive in the current cycle?
A: Reconstructing context. The same types of failures come back, but the surrounding evidence is always scattered across files, emails, and memory.

    Q: Which defect type is worst operationally?
A: The ones that look small but require detective work, especially identifier or classification issues. They are rarely a single-cell fix because they expose a broken relationship.

    Q: What would save you the most time immediately?
A: Better triage. If the system already knew the likely owner, the rule severity, the historical pattern, and the suggested remediation path, my time would shift from hunting to deciding.

    Q: What needs to remain human today?
A: Anything that changes interpretation, anything that changes more than one record in a structurally important way, and anything that might create a mismatch with other reporting frameworks.

    Q: What evidence matters most to operations?
A: Before-and-after values, the reason for the change, and whether the same problem was seen last month. Without that, root-cause work keeps resetting.

    Q: When would you trust an automated workflow?
A: When the system is boring in the right way. It should catch routine issues consistently, route them clearly, and let me override only with a named reason.

    
    
    Chief Data Officer

    Q: Where does the data problem really sit?
A: In ownership gaps. The bank often has data, but not a stable steward for the meaning of the data once it crosses systems and transformations.

    Q: What concerns you most about the current model?
A: Shared fields with no true owner. Those are the fields that look governed until an exception appears, then everyone discovers the stewardship boundary was implicit.

    Q: Why is the one-loan trace so useful?
A: Because it forces specificity. It prevents vague statements like “the source is T24” and turns them into actual field-level accountability.

    Q: What would you want from the workshop output?
A: A critical-data inventory for AnaCredit, mapped to stewards, transformations, and evidence sources. I would also want a clear distinction between source-of-truth values and policy-derived values.

    Q: What trust threshold do you apply to AI recommendations?
A: They are useful when they improve diagnosis. They are risky when they blur the difference between observed data and inferred classification.

    Q: What tells you the future design is mature enough for engineering?
A: When every critical attribute has a source, a steward, a transformation owner, and a rule path. Without that, engineering is being asked to encode ambiguity.

    
    
    Chief Risk Officer

    Q: Why does this matter from a risk perspective?
A: Because weak reporting controls contaminate the risk narrative. If the bank cannot explain the data pipeline behind a regulatory number, confidence in the control environment falls quickly.

    Q: What is the biggest risk in an “agentic” framing?
A: Confusing acceleration with delegation of accountability. The bank can accelerate decisions, but it cannot outsource accountability to a model.

    Q: Which decisions should stay human-approved?
A: Material overrides, interpretive classifications, acceptance of unresolved warnings, and any decision that changes the bank’s risk story across frameworks.

    Q: What evidence do you need as CRO?
A: I need to see severity, impact, owner, mitigation, and rationale. For anything material, I also need to see whether similar issues have recurred.

    Q: What would increase your confidence in the platform?
A: Hard separation between deterministic rules and probabilistic suggestions. If those lines blur, challenge becomes very difficult.

    Q: What would make the first release unacceptable?
A: A workflow that is faster but less legible. Speed without auditability is not a win in a regulated control process.

    
    
    Lending Ops Owner

    Q: How do AnaCredit issues usually reach your team today?
A: Late and compressed. We get asked for an explanation when the central process is already under time pressure, which means sensible source fixes are suddenly treated like urgent exceptions.

    Q: What do reporting teams often misunderstand about source systems?
A: A regulatory field can look simple at reporting level but still be product-dependent or over-loaded operationally. The source system was not always built around the regulator’s logic.

    Q: What do you need from the future process?
A: Early visibility and structured requests. If I can see the rule, the specific field, the affected population, and the downstream consequence, I can respond much faster.

    Q: What should never happen automatically?
A: Any change that effectively rewrites business meaning in the source without domain review. Suggested fixes are fine; silent upstream mutation is not.

    Q: What evidence matters to your team?
A: The exact source field, the current population count, and whether the issue is recurring. Otherwise it is hard to justify source-system prioritization.

    Q: When would you trust the new system?
A: When it helps us fix problems at the right layer instead of turning every issue into a reporting-only workaround.

    
    
    Internal Audit Lead

    Q: What is your first question when reviewing a new control workflow?
A: Can I replay it? If the answer is no, I already know the process is relying on memory or informal evidence somewhere.

    Q: What typically goes wrong in evidence packs?
A: They prove the final number but not the control story. You can see what was reported, but not which rule was applied, who overrode what, or which version was in force.

    Q: What would you expect from the future Trust Record?
A: Snapshot ID, policy version, rule results, approval logs, override reasons, submission artifact, and feedback linkage back to the run.

    Q: What do you care about in the human-in-the-loop design?
A: Segregation of duties and clarity of authority. If the same person can author, approve, and attest without a compensating control, the workflow is fragile.

    Q: How do you view AI-generated suggestions?
A: As advisory content that requires strong provenance. If the recommendation is visible but supported by nothing, it adds narrative, not assurance.

    Q: What would make the project audit-ready?
A: Not perfection. Reproducibility, completeness of evidence, stable access control, and a clear answer to why the system made the decision it made.

    
    
    Supervisor Liaison

    Q: What does the external conversation feel like when the bank receives a correction request?
A: It becomes a credibility test immediately. The issue is rarely only the value; it is whether the bank can respond clearly and consistently.

    Q: What information do you most want available when talking to the regulator?
A: The rule context, the affected scope, whether it is deterministic or interpretive, the remediation path, and the expected timing for correction.

    Q: What weakens confidence most quickly?
A: Different teams telling different versions of the same story. That usually means the evidence is fragmented or the ownership model is not settled.

    Q: What would a better response pack contain?
A: A concise issue summary, run identifier, source lineage, corrective action, named approver, and whether the issue indicates a one-off defect or a structural weakness.

    Q: How should the system treat regulator feedback?
A: As structured input, not just an email prompt. The item should be classified, routed, linked to the run, and converted into either a source fix, a policy backlog item, or a governance action.

    Q: What would increase trust in the demo?
A: If it can show the full response chain from feedback receipt to root cause to approved correction without any manual reconstruction.

    
    
    Executive Sponsor

    Q: Why sponsor this now?
A: Because AnaCredit is a practical place to prove whether the bank can modernize a regulated workflow safely. It is specific enough to build, but important enough to matter.

    Q: What do you need from the workshop?
A: Not inspiration alone. I need a problem statement, metrics, decision rights, build priorities, and clarity on what the first release will actually prove.

    Q: What is unacceptable in the first build?
A: A visually impressive demo that still depends on unresolved authority, unclear owners, or hidden manual work.

    Q: What evidence would justify continued funding?
A: Measurable reduction in manual effort and cycle time, better first-pass quality, and visible improvement in control evidence.

    Q: What is your trust threshold for automated decisioning?
A: The system can automate prepared control work. It cannot obscure accountability or remove the need for explicit approval in material cases.

    Q: Why is this more than one use case?
A: Because the reusable asset is the governed operating pattern: policy, agents, approval, evidence, and replay. AnaCredit is only the lighthouse.

    
    
    IT Integration Lead

    Q: Where is the biggest technical risk?
A: In hidden checkpoints. Manual uploads and handoffs are doing control work today, but that work is undocumented. Once we automate them, we have to replace the control deliberately.

    Q: What do discovery workshops often miss?
A: Versioning. Schema versioning, rule versioning, payload versioning, and release timing. Those details determine whether a workflow is stable or brittle.

    Q: What do you need before substantial build starts?
A: Contracted source payloads, a golden dataset, exception taxonomy, rule catalog, and a clear approval model. Otherwise engineering is building around assumptions.

    Q: What must the runtime support from day one?
A: Replay, rollback, observability, and a run ID that ties together payload, rule set, decisions, and evidence.

    Q: How do you see AI suggestions technically?
A: As an advisory service behind a controlled interface. They should never be confused with the authoritative policy result.

    Q: What would convince you the workflow is production-grade?
A: If I can inspect a run end to end, reproduce the decision path, and reverse a release safely when a local instruction or schema changes.

    
    
    Product and Engineering Owner

    Q: What is your biggest scope risk?
A: Trying to build the full target architecture before the rule authority and artifact contracts are stable. That usually creates attractive demos and weak production foundations.

    Q: How would you structure the first release?
A: Deterministic validation first, visible lineage, human review, and evidence capture. Reconciliation and learning should follow behind that, not in front of it.

    Q: What do you need from the workshop that is most often missing?
A: Acceptance criteria stated in business language but testable in engineering language. The Demo Contract should be clear enough to drive backlog slicing.

    Q: What should be in the definition of done?
A: Built, tested, logged, explainable, approved, and replayable. In this use case, “works on sample data” is not enough.

    Q: How should unresolved questions be handled?
A: Logged publicly with owner, impact, and due date. Hidden ambiguity is the fastest way to slow delivery later.

    Q: What tells you the workshop succeeded?
A: When engineering can start with no guesswork about source hierarchy, data contracts, approval boundaries, or evidence requirements.
