Alpitour AI Lab Co-Creation Workshop for EasyBook Next and AlpiGPT
Executive summary
This workshop should not be run as a generic “AI ideation session.” It should be run as a decision-oriented validation workshop for a very specific proposition: can an embedded AlpiGPT concierge help external travel agents move from traveler brief to quote, alternative recovery, proposal, hold, and modification with materially less dependence on Alpitour support teams? The design center is therefore the B2B travel-agent workflow, not consumer chat, and the most important outputs are evidence about autonomy, trust, orchestration clarity, and embedded front-end fit inside EasyBook Next. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file1
The strongest two-hour format is a mixed-persona, highly interactive workshop that combines four modes in sequence: explicit success framing, live roleplay against the deterministic demo, low-to-mid-fidelity front-end prototyping, and AI-assisted scenario stress testing. That sequence is supported by three complementary method families. Google’s PAIR work offers the right critique lenses for human-AI systems, especially user needs, mental models, explainability, control, and graceful failure; frog contributes excellent collaboration mechanics for cross-functional, possibly remote sessions; and IDEO contributes the most useful prototyping stance for AI-enabled products: start with intention, keep human-centered values visible, rethink fidelity, and use roleplay and experimentation instead of over-polished artifacts. [1]
Because the current demo is a deterministic React prototype with no real APIs, the workshop should not try to validate live pricing truth, inventory truth, or systems integration. Instead, it should validate the things a prototype can legitimately test: whether participants understand what the agent is doing, whether they can see when to trust it and when to escalate, whether sold-out recovery preserves traveler intent, whether proposal and hold flows are commercially useful, whether modification is self-service enough to reduce support dependency, and whether the embedded UI behaves like a serious travel-sales workbench rather than “just another chat panel.” That distinction is important in human-AI design research, which shows that collaboration improves when people understand AI behavior, can calibrate reliance, and can interrogate limitations rather than treating the system as an opaque oracle. fileciteturn0file1 [2]
If well facilitated, the workshop should leave the AI Lab with five concrete outputs: a ranked persona-and-metrics board, an annotated journey and orchestration map, a set of embedded EasyBook Next front-end concepts, a scored rubric across the demo beats, and a prioritized backlog for Demo v2. That is enough to give leadership a credible value story, product/design a next-sprint scope, support teams a clearer escalation boundary, and the AI Lab audience a stronger proof point that the concept is agentic workflow orchestration, not only question answering. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file1
Research grounding
The workshop design should borrow from three distinct but complementary evidence bases.
Source family
What it contributes
Actionable takeaway for this workshop
Google PAIR
The official People + AI Guidebook is a major industry resource for human-centered AI. A comparative analysis identifies six PAIR concept areas: user needs and success definition, data collection and evaluation, mental models, explainability and trust, feedback and control, and errors and graceful failure. A later study of 31 practitioners found teams used the guidebook for education, cross-functional communication, and internal resources, but wanted more help with early ideation and problem formulation. [3]
Use PAIR as the evaluation lens, not the whole workshop. Start by making success metrics explicit, then test mental models, trust, control, and failure handling in each activity.
frog
frog recommends structuring collaboration in the order outcome, team, process, tools. It also emphasizes a supportive creative climate, mixed perspectives, strong facilitation, and digital-canvas practices for remote collaboration; it explicitly suggests methods such as Person in the Pit, Mental Excursion, and Intrigue Selection for framing, engagement, and down-selection. [4]
Make the first 15 minutes about outcomes and evidence; assign a board navigator/tool sherpa; use one divergent exercise and one convergent exercise only; keep selection criteria visible.
IDEO
IDEO frames design thinking as a human-centered, iterative practice of testing hypotheses and building on findings. IDEO U’s AI materials emphasize starting with intention, rethinking fidelity, roleplaying with AI, co-designing with people, and balancing AI-generated with human-centered design. Their live AI workshop materials also foreground prompt engineering, responsible AI use, data synthesis, and knowing when to use AI in the design process. [5]
Keep prototypes rough; force participants to articulate intention before screen changes; make roleplay central; use AI as a crit partner and stress-testing tool, not a replacement for human judgment.
A second layer of research sharpens how the workshop should test an agentic product. Recent HCI work on generative AI prototyping finds that collaborative teams increasingly use content-centric prototyping: they start with the content they want the system to produce, identify attributes and constraints, and iterate prompts and examples together. That is extremely relevant here, because the Alpitour demo’s value depends on whether proposal content, alternative rationales, and modifications are commercially useful and legible to a travel agent. [6]
Other studies reinforce the need to make the agent’s strengths, limits, and boundaries visible. Showing behavior descriptions helps people identify AI failures and rely on the system when it is more accurate; natural-language onboarding rules can improve human-AI team performance; and UX designers need better support for model understanding and transparency in order to ideate responsibly. In practice, that means the workshop should produce not only UI concepts, but also a reliance rules card and a known-strengths / known-limits panel for the embedded experience. [7]
Finally, a 2026 CHI workshop paper argues that LLM agents should often be designed as remote collaborators, not passive tools, because trust, awareness, common ground, and explicit signaling become central in human-agent work. That is a useful frame for this session: the question is not merely “did the chatbot answer?”, but “can the travel agent establish enough common ground with the system to safely delegate parts of the sales workflow?” [8]
Workshop architecture
The workshop should be framed around three objectives that match the lab-visit context and the prototype’s current maturity. First, validate the agentic orchestration story across the sales journey beats the demo already implies. Second, test support-deflection potential in the moments that currently trigger calls or tickets. Third, envision the embedded UI workbench that makes the orchestration trustworthy and operationally usable inside EasyBook Next. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file1

These thresholds should be treated as workshop quality gates, not production KPIs. That distinction matters because a deterministic prototype can test comprehension, orchestration legibility, and task fit, but not live operational performance. PAIR’s emphasis on success definition, mental models, feedback/control, and graceful failure is exactly why this workshop should separate experience validation from system-truth validation. [10]
Constraints that should shape facilitation
The workshop has four material constraints. The first is technical: no live APIs, so “availability,” “pricing,” and “policies” are simulated. The second is temporal: two hours is enough for validation and divergence, but not for a full service blueprint. The third is environmental: the format must work either in-person or remote. The fourth is narrative: this is an embedded B2B sales flow, so the front-end concept should behave like a travel-sales workbench with provenance, actions, and proposal controls, not just a chat pane. fileciteturn0file1

Model-agnostic prompt pack
The prompt pack below is written to work in any strong LLM interface. It is most useful if the AI operator feeds the model only the scenario cards, mock product facts, and prototype constraints. That keeps the session grounded and avoids accidental invention. This approach aligns with content-centric prototyping research and with IDEO U’s emphasis on prompt engineering, responsible use, and balancing AI-generated with human-centered design. [14]
Activity
Prompt
What to look for
Persona and KPI alignment
You are a workshop synthesis assistant. Input: prioritized personas, desired business outcomes, and prototype constraints. Output: five testable workshop questions, each with one observable signal and one failure condition. Keep the questions specific to a B2B travel agent selling a package holiday.
Whether the room is aligned on evidence, not slogans.
Roleplay quote and hold
Act as a travel customer visiting an agency. Generate a realistic brief for a family asking for an all-inclusive Egypt holiday in August. Include stated preferences, one hidden constraint, and one point likely to require clarification. Do not solve the trip.
Whether AlpiGPT asks the right questions before proposing options.
Sold-out recovery
Simulate an agentic concierge handling a sold-out preferred hotel. Preserve traveler intent. For each proposed alternative, label the rationale as: exact match, near match, commercial compromise, or unsupported guess. Then state when the system should recommend an escalation to support.
Whether recovery preserves intent and trust instead of simply filling the screen with alternatives.
Front-end prototyping
Given an embedded travel-selling interface inside EasyBook Next, propose a low-to-mid-fidelity workbench with these zones: traveler brief, grounding/provenance, proposal workspace, action rail, and escalation state. Output only panel names, user actions, and what must remain editable by the agent.
Whether the concept behaves like a workbench, not a chat log.
Modification testing
Using only the scripted scenario data, generate three post-proposal change requests: a simple date change, a hotel substitution, and a risky request that should trigger escalation. For each, specify what the user needs to see to trust the change.
Whether modification logic is legible and self-service boundaries are clear.
Workshop synthesis
Summarize workshop notes into Keep / Change / Open Questions. For each item, map it to persona, demo beat, and business metric. Highlight any issue that would block leadership confidence in support deflection.
Whether feedback converts cleanly into backlog-ready decisions.
A small but important addition is a reliance card prompt, inspired by human-AI onboarding research:
Draft three plain-language rules for when a travel agent should trust the concierge, when they should verify, and when they should escalate. Base the rules only on the current prototype’s known constraints.
That kind of natural-language guidance helps participants reason about appropriate reliance instead of treating the system as universally authoritative. [15]

The artifact set should be deliberately lean. The point is not to leave with perfect documentation; it is to leave with decision-grade evidence. The minimum useful package is: one photo/export per board, one scored rubric per group, one consolidated backlog, and one short leadership narrative that links findings back to the four business metrics. frog’s emphasis on contribution being visible and meaningful is a good reminder here: if participants cannot see how their outputs will change the next demo, the session will feel performative. The workshop should be considered successful if it produces all of the following: a shared primary-persona focus, evidence-backed judgments on support-deflection potential, one converged embedded front-end direction, explicit escalation boundaries, and a small set of high-confidence prototype changes tied to business metrics. If the room instead leaves with only generalized enthusiasm for “AI opportunities,” the workshop has failed its purpose.
